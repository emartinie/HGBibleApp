Biblical Hebrew Biblical Hebrew is: root-based symbolic layered concrete relational Words are often built from 3-letter roots that connect families of ideas together. Example: שָׁמַע (shama) means: hear listen obey English separates those into different concepts. Hebrew sees them as connected realities. So when Scripture says: “Hear, O Israel…” the implication is not merely “audibly detect sound.” It means: receive internalize respond faithfully That’s not “less precise.” It’s actually more integrated. Greek Koine Greek is often: analytical philosophical structurally precise conceptually nuanced Greek can distinguish between shades of meaning English collapses together. Classic example: ἀγάπη (agapē) φιλία (philia) ἔρως (eros) English says: love Greek asks: What kind? Covenantal? Brotherly? Romantic? Sacrificial? That’s increased precision in categorization. But English has strengths too English is: massively flexible adaptive hybridized context-rich technologically evolved English can absorb vocabulary from: Hebrew Greek Latin Germanic languages French Arabic and more That gives it enormous expressive range. The weakness is: modern English often favors efficiency and simplification theological vocabulary became institutionalized many ancient concepts were translated into flattened church terminology So sometimes English loses: covenantal texture layered symbolism poetic ambiguity root connections The deeper issue is worldview This is the real thing you keep bumping into. Hebrew thinking is often: relational covenantal embodied pattern-oriented Greek thinking is often: categorical logical metaphysical analytical Modern Western English thinking is often: industrial reductionist efficiency-driven disconnected from symbolic structure So sometimes the issue is not the language itself. It’s the civilization using it. Which connects directly to Jacob’s Ladder Jacob's Ladder is a perfect example. English readers often imagine: a literal ladder to heaven Hebrew readers encounter: connection between heaven and earth movement between realms covenant continuity ascent/descent sacred space divine encounter transformation mediation Then Jesus of Nazareth references it in John 1: angels ascending and descending upon the Son of Man He is effectively presenting himself as: the meeting point the bridge the access point the living temple the pathway between heaven and earth Which is exactly why your instinct about: journeys paths ascent growth steps teaching ladders discipleship progression …actually fits the biblical imagery remarkably well.
