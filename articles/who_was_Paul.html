Who was Paul (Rav Shaul)?

1. Historical identity
Hebrew name: Sha’ul (שאול) / Saul
Roman name: Paulus (Paul)
From: Tarsus in Cilicia (modern southern Turkey)
Ethnicity: Jewish
Legal status: Roman citizen (very important politically and socially)
He is one of the few early Jewish figures we can triangulate through:
his own letters (primary source)
Acts of the Apostles (secondary narrative source)
later tradition

2. Education and formation

Paul explicitly identifies himself as:
“a Pharisee, son of Pharisees” (Acts 23:6) Trained under Gamaliel (Acts 22:3)

Why that matters:

Gamaliel was associated with:
the Pharisaic tradition of Torah interpretation a more measured legal approach (see Acts 5)

So Paul is not:
a random convert
a Gentile outsider
a “new religion starter” in his own mind

He is:
a deeply trained Torah interpreter operating inside Second Temple Jewish frameworks

3. What he originally believed Before following Jesus/ Yeshua: He was a zealous Pharisee Concerned with protecting covenant fidelity Opposed to the early Jesus movement (seen as sectarian / destabilizing)

He describes himself as:
“blameless under the law” (Philippians 3:6 — within his interpretive system)

4. The turning point (Damascus experience)
Paul describes a dramatic encounter with the risen Jesus/ Yeshua (multiple retellings in Acts + references in his letters).

Core outcome:
He becomes convinced Jesus/ Yeshua is: the Jewish Messiah vindicated by resurrection central to God’s unfolding plan for the house of Israel and the house of Judah

Important nuance:
Paul does NOT describe this even once, as leaving Judaism He frames it as re-interpretation of Israel’s story to include the grafting in of gentiles

5. What Paul actually did

He became:

A missionary to Gentiles

Not rejecting Jews
But focusing on non-Jews being included
Founder of assemblies (ekklesia)
Not “church buildings”
House-based communities
Writer of early theological letters

His letters include:

Romans
Corinthians
Galatians
Philippians
Thessalonians
Philemon

These are:

the earliest Christian texts we possess

6. Central theological ideas (simplified but accurate)

1. Messiah-centered interpretation of Torah/ Penteteuch

Paul reads Scripture through Jesus/ Yeshua.

2. Inclusion of Gentiles

A major issue:

Do Gentiles need full Torah conversion?

Paul’s answer (same as Jesus/ Yeshua) (simplified):

Gentiles are included because of Messiah along with the house of Israel
Not required to become ethnically Jewish (as if that were possible)

This is the most debated point in all of Paul studies.

3. “Faith” (πίστις) or (אֱמוּנָה)

Often misunderstood.

It is closer to:

loyalty
trust
allegiance

Not just mental belief.

4. “Law” (Torah)

Paul’s language is complex:

sometimes praises Torah
sometimes critiques misuse of Torah as identity boundary

He is NOT simply “anti-Torah”

He is:

arguing about function, identity, and covenant context

7. Major misunderstanding point

Many modern readers assume:

Paul = anti-Jewish
Paul = created Christianity separate from Judaism
Paul = replaced Torah with “new religion”

But historically:

Paul remains inside Jewish categories
He debates how Gentiles join Israel’s God
He expects fulfillment of Israel’s promises, not replacement

8. His identity tension (this is the core tension of his writings)

Paul holds all of these at once:

Jewish Pharisee
follower of Jesus/ Yeshua
apostle to Gentiles
apocalyptic thinker (end-times framework)
Torah interpreter
Roman citizen operating in empire structures

That tension is what makes him hard to read.

9. Death

Tradition holds:

executed in Rome (likely under Nero, ~60–64 AD)
No solid historical dispute on martyrdom tradition, though exact details are not fully verifiable.
