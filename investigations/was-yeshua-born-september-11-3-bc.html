Was Yeshua Born on September 11, 3 BC?

A careful investigation of the Gospel narratives, Herod’s chronology, the star of Bethlehem, Revelation 12, and the proposed astronomical configuration.

Does the evidence identify September 11, 3 BC—or does that date depend upon a chain of interpretations that remains possible, disputed, or unproven?

Method notice: This page is an investigation, not a declaration. It distinguishes what a source says explicitly, what may reasonably be inferred, what belongs to a proposed reconstruction, and what remains open. A precise date will not be claimed unless the textual, historical, chronological, and astronomical evidence can carry it together.
Investigation Question

What would have to be true?

The claim “Yeshua was born on September 11, 3 BC” is not one claim. It joins several separate questions. Each must be tested on its own before the whole chronology can be assessed.

The Gospel record

What do Matthew and Luke actually say, and what chronological clues do they preserve?

Herod’s death

Does the surviving evidence place the death of Herod the Great in 4 BC, 1 BC, or another year?

The star

Was the star a conjunction, comet, nova, symbolic sign, supernatural event, or something else?

Revelation 12

Does the woman, child, sun, moon, and stars describe Messiah’s birth, another event, or several layers at once?

The sky calculation

Did the proposed arrangement of Virgo, the sun, the moon, Jupiter, Regulus, and the other bodies occur on the stated date?

The dependency chain

How much of the date depends on Revelation 12, and how much depends on a 1 BC death of Herod?

Reading discipline

Four evidence labels

These labels prevent a plausible reconstruction from quietly becoming an explicit statement of the source.

Explicit

The source states it

Example: Matthew places Yeshua’s birth during the reign of Herod the king (Matthew 2:1).

Inferred

The source permits a conclusion

Example: Herod’s order concerning boys two years old and under may imply a time range, but it does not state Yeshua’s exact age.

Proposed

A reconstruction joins several clues

Example: identifying Revelation 12 with a specific sky configuration and then using that configuration to select one calendar date.

Open

The evidence has not settled it

Example: the physical nature of the star of Bethlehem remains unidentified by the Gospel text itself.

Primary texts

1. What Matthew and Luke actually tell us

Neither Gospel gives a calendar date for Yeshua’s birth. Both place the birth in identifiable historical settings, but they preserve different details and must first be heard separately.

Matthew 1–2

Explicit observations

  • Yeshua is born in Bethlehem of Judea in the days of Herod the king (Matthew 2:1).
  • Magi from the east report seeing “his star” and come to honor the child (Matthew 2:1–2).
  • Herod asks when the star appeared and later orders the death of boys two years old and under according to the time learned from the Magi (Matthew 2:7, 16).
  • The family flees to Egypt and returns after Herod’s death (Matthew 2:13–21).
Luke 1–2

Explicit observations

  • The birth occurs in the reign of Caesar Augustus and is connected to a registration associated with Quirinius (Luke 2:1–2).
  • Joseph and Miriam travel from Nazareth to Bethlehem, where Yeshua is born (Luke 2:3–7).
  • Shepherds are outdoors keeping watch over their flock by night (Luke 2:8).
  • Yeshua is circumcised on the eighth day and later presented at the Temple according to Torah (Luke 2:21–24).
  • Luke 3:1 and 3:23 later place the beginning of Yeshua’s ministry in the fifteenth year of Tiberius, when he was “about thirty.”
What the Gospel texts do not state
  • Neither Matthew nor Luke names a month, day, festival, season, or year for the birth.
  • Matthew does not identify the star as a planet, conjunction, comet, nova, or constellation.
  • Luke does not state how long shepherds remained in the fields during that year.
  • Neither Gospel connects the birth explicitly to Revelation 12.
  • Neither Gospel says the Magi arrived on the night of the birth.
First control: A September 11, 3 BC birth is therefore a reconstruction, not an explicit Gospel date. That does not make it false. It determines what kind of claim it is and what additional evidence it must carry.
Chronological clues

2. Which clues can constrain the date?

Herod the Great

What is explicit: Yeshua was born before Herod’s death in Matthew’s sequence.

What must be inferred or reconstructed: The calendar year of Herod’s death and the interval between the birth, the Magi, Egypt, and Herod’s death.

The star and Magi

What is explicit: The Magi saw a sign they called “his star”; Herod learned its time from them.

What must be inferred or reconstructed: The object’s identity, first appearance, duration, motion, and connection to a particular astronomical event.

Herod’s two-year order

What is explicit: The order used a two-year-and-under range related to information from the Magi.

What must be inferred or reconstructed: Whether the range reflects Yeshua’s age, Herod’s caution, the star’s first appearance, or some combination.

Augustus and Quirinius

What is explicit: Luke links the birth to Augustus, a registration, and Quirinius.

What must be inferred or reconstructed: Which administrative event Luke means and how it fits the known Syrian and Judean chronology.

Tiberius and “about thirty”

What is explicit: Luke dates John’s activity to Tiberius’s fifteenth year and calls Yeshua “about thirty.”

What must be inferred or reconstructed: How broadly to read “about,” how to count Tiberius’s years, and the interval before Yeshua’s baptism.

Priestly service of Zechariah

What is explicit: Zechariah belonged to the division of Abijah; pregnancies and births follow in Luke 1.

What must be inferred or reconstructed: The division’s service date in that particular year and whether an unbroken schedule can be reconstructed.

Shepherds at night

What is explicit: Shepherds were keeping watch outdoors when the announcement came.

What must be inferred or reconstructed: What months this practice allows or excludes in the Bethlehem region.

Disputed chronology

3. When did Herod the Great die?

This question is load-bearing. Matthew places the birth before Herod’s death, so a 3 BC birth requires Herod to have lived beyond 3 BC.

Traditional view

Herod dies in 4 BC

This reconstruction commonly reads Josephus’s regnal notices, the succession of Herod’s sons, Roman-era synchronisms, and the lunar eclipse shortly before Herod’s death together. It usually identifies Josephus’s eclipse with the partial lunar eclipse of March 13, 4 BC, followed by Passover and the transition to Herod’s successors.

If correct, Yeshua’s birth must precede 4 BC, usually producing proposals around 6–4 BC.

Minority view

Herod dies in 1 BC

This reconstruction questions parts of the conventional regnal-year calculation and identifies Josephus’s eclipse with a later eclipse, often the one of January 10, 1 BC. It argues that the longer interval before Passover better fits the many events Josephus places between the eclipse and Herod’s funeral and succession.

If correct, a birth in 3 or 2 BC remains chronologically possible within Matthew’s Herodian setting.

Primary evidence docket for the Herod question
  • Josephus, Antiquities 17: Herod’s final illness, the lunar eclipse, his death, funeral, succession, and the Passover disturbances.
  • Josephus, Jewish War 1–2: the parallel narrative and its chronological details.
  • Regnal evidence: coins, inscriptions, and the dated reigns of Archelaus, Antipas, and Philip.
  • Roman chronology: events tied to Augustus, Syria, Judea, and the confirmation of Herod’s successors.
  • Astronomical calculation: every lunar eclipse visible from Judea in the plausible date range, including magnitude, timing, visibility, and interval before Passover.
No verdict entered yet: The eclipse alone cannot date Herod’s death. The strongest chronology must explain Josephus’s complete sequence, the regnal evidence, the Roman synchronisms, and the available time before Passover.
Identity unresolved

4. What was the “star of Bethlehem”?

Matthew describes the star’s significance and role in the Magi’s journey, but does not identify its physical mechanism. Each proposal must explain both the astronomical evidence and Matthew’s language.

Planetary conjunction

Tests whether repeated or close planetary meetings could have carried royal or Judean significance to eastern observers.

Comet

Tests ancient records of visible comets and whether a comet fits the timing, language, and cultural meaning of Matthew’s account.

Nova or supernova

Tests records of a new or guest star and asks whether it could remain visible long enough to fit the journey.

Planetary station or retrograde motion

Tests whether the apparent stopping of a planet could illuminate Matthew’s statement that the star “stood over” a location.

Symbolic or literary sign

Tests the star in relation to biblical royal imagery, Balaam’s oracle, and ancient birth narratives without assuming a recoverable object.

Miraculous or otherwise unidentified

Allows that Matthew may describe an event that ordinary astronomical models cannot reproduce.

Control question: Does a proposed object merely occur near the right period, or does it explain why the Magi read it as the birth of the king of the Jews?
Interpretive question

5. Does Revelation 12 describe the birth of Messiah?

Revelation 12 presents a woman clothed with the sun, the moon under her feet, a crown of twelve stars, a male child destined to rule the nations, a dragon, conflict in heaven, and the woman’s flight into the wilderness.

Textual observation

The child is messianic

The language of ruling the nations with a rod of iron echoes Psalm 2 and strongly identifies the child with Messiah.

Corporate symbolism

The woman exceeds one individual

The imagery may draw from Israel, Zion, the people of God, and Genesis 37. Miriam may be included in the birth image without exhausting the woman’s identity.

Historical layer

Messiah’s birth may be in view

The birth and threat from the dragon may compress Yeshua’s coming, opposition, victory, and exaltation into an apocalyptic scene.

Astronomical layer

A sky configuration is proposed

Some readers map the woman to Virgo and the sun, moon, and stars to an observable arrangement. Revelation itself does not supply a calendar conversion or name Virgo.

Working assessment: Revelation 12 can contain historical, prophetic, and symbolic dimensions at the same time. Demonstrating a messianic birth layer is not yet the same as demonstrating that the vision encodes September 11, 3 BC.
Calculation required

6. Did the proposed configuration occur on September 11, 3 BC?

This claim should be tested with a reproducible astronomical calculation, not a screenshot whose settings are unknown. The civil calendar, year numbering, location, time of day, horizon, coordinate system, and visibility conditions must all be recorded.

Virgo

Claim to test: The woman can be represented by the constellation Virgo.

Evidence required: Constellation boundaries or ancient star figure used, plus the observer’s location and time.

Sun

Claim to test: The woman was “clothed with the sun.”

Evidence required: Solar longitude and position relative to Virgo across the proposed date window.

Moon

Claim to test: The moon was “under her feet.”

Evidence required: Lunar position hour by hour, including what counts as the figure’s feet and whether the moon was above the horizon.

Jupiter

Claim to test: Jupiter functioned as a royal or messianic planet and participated in the sign.

Evidence required: Its exact position, apparent motion, conjunctions, stations, visibility, and ancient interpretive basis.

Regulus

Claim to test: Jupiter’s relation to Regulus contributed royal symbolism.

Evidence required: Angular separations and dates for the full conjunction sequence, not only one selected frame.

Twelve-star crown

Claim to test: Stars and/or planets above Virgo supplied a crown of twelve.

Evidence required: A non-arbitrary count, with every included body named and the same counting rule applied consistently.

Calendar date

Claim to test: The configuration corresponds to September 11, 3 BC and possibly Tishri 1.

Evidence required: Documented conversion among astronomical year numbering, Julian dating, local sunset boundaries, and the reconstructed Hebrew calendar.

Minimum reproducibility record
  1. Name and version of the astronomical software or ephemeris.
  2. Observer location, coordinates, elevation, and local time basis.
  3. Calendar system and treatment of the missing year zero in BC/AD notation.
  4. Precession, atmospheric refraction, horizon, and daylight settings.
  5. Coordinates or angular separations for each claimed body.
  6. A date window around the proposed day to test whether the arrangement is unique.
  7. An independent calculation using a second ephemeris or tool.
Evidence status: No astronomical verdict is entered in this first working file. The configuration must be calculated, documented, and independently reproduced before it can support the date.
Dependency test

7. How much does the September 11 theory depend on other disputed claims?

Revelation 12

Date-selection dependency

The exact September date depends heavily on reading Revelation 12 as an observable birth configuration and mapping its imagery to particular celestial bodies.

Herod dies 1 BC

Historical-permission dependency

A 3 BC birth depends on Herod still being alive afterward. A 4 BC death would exclude the proposed date within Matthew’s chronology.

Astronomical event

Occurrence dependency

Even a real configuration establishes that the sky looked a certain way. Additional evidence is required to identify it as the event Revelation or Matthew intended.

Calendar conversion

Named-date dependency

The modern date and any identification with Tishri 1 depend on the accuracy of ancient calendar reconstruction and sunset-based day boundaries.

Result: The theory is cumulative. Its precision is only as strong as its weakest necessary link. A piece of evidence may remain valuable even if the complete September 11 conclusion does not follow.
Evidence docket

8. Begin with evidence—not internet opinions

The investigation should preserve the source, the observation drawn from it, and the confidence assigned to that observation.

Matthew and Luke

Read the birth narratives in full, compare their sequence carefully, and record explicit chronological statements before harmonizing them.

Josephus

Collect the complete final-Herod narratives in Antiquities and Jewish War, including the eclipse, illness, executions, funeral, succession, and Passover.

Roman chronology

Collect inscriptions, coins, dated offices, and administrative records relevant to Augustus, Quirinius, Syria, Judea, and Herod’s successors.

Lunar eclipses

Calculate all plausible eclipses visible from Judea and compare their timing with Josephus’s complete narrative.

Astronomical ephemerides

Use documented calculations to test the sun, moon, planets, stars, conjunctions, stations, visibility, and date conversion.

Dead Sea Scroll calendars

Use only where relevant to first-century Jewish calendar practice; do not assume a sectarian calendar governed the Gospel chronology.

Second Temple expectations

Collect Jewish royal, messianic, celestial, and apocalyptic texts that may illuminate how signs could be understood in the period.

Early Christian writers

Record the earliest surviving birth-date and chronology traditions, their dates, their sources, and how far removed they are from the events.

Rabbinic chronology

Consult carefully, distinguishing the date of the recorded tradition from the date of the event it describes.

Competing Views

9. Let each major position state its strongest case

Traditional chronology

Birth around 6–4 BC

Herod dies in 4 BC. Matthew therefore requires a birth before that date. The exact day and the physical identity of the star remain unknown.

Must explain: Luke’s registration language, the full Herodian sequence, and the range of proposed earlier astronomical events.

September 11, 3 BC chronology

Birth on or near Tishri 1

Herod dies in 1 BC. Revelation 12 reflects an actual celestial configuration, and that configuration identifies the birth date while royal planetary activity supplies wider context for the Magi.

Must explain: why Revelation 12 functions as a date code, why 1 BC best fits Herod, and why the proposed sky arrangement is both accurate and historically relevant.

Spring proposals

Passover season or pastoral setting

These proposals emphasize shepherding conditions, lambing, Passover symbolism, priestly schedules, or alternative chronological calculations.

Must explain: the historical date range and whether the seasonal clues are firm enough to identify a month.

Sukkot proposals

Autumn festival symbolism

These proposals connect the incarnation with dwelling or tabernacling imagery, festival timing, priestly courses, or the crowded conditions in Bethlehem.

Must explain: whether the symbolism establishes chronology or only theological resonance.

Date unknown

The sources do not preserve enough precision

This position accepts the historical setting of the birth narratives while concluding that the surviving evidence cannot recover an exact day.

Must explain: whether cumulative chronological and astronomical evidence can narrow the date more than the texts do explicitly.

Current assessment

What can be said before the evidence is collected?

September 11, 3 BC is a testable historical-astronomical proposal, but it is not a date stated by Matthew or Luke. Its viability depends especially on the date of Herod’s death, the interpretation of Revelation 12, a reproducible sky calculation, and the legitimacy of joining those pieces together.

The traditional 6–4 BC range also depends on reconstruction, especially the 4 BC Herod chronology. Responsible investigation should therefore compare complete cases rather than treating either chronology as self-evident.

Version 1 assessment: The exact date remains open. The next step is not to defend or dismiss September 11, 3 BC. It is to build a transparent evidence file in which every source, calculation, inference, and dependency can be checked.
Questions Still Open

Questions Still Open

  1. Is Revelation 12 intended to preserve a historical birth sign, or does it use birth imagery for a broader apocalyptic message?
  2. Which Herod chronology best explains all of Josephus, the regnal evidence, and the Roman synchronisms?
  3. Which lunar eclipse best fits Josephus’s narrative, and is the time between that eclipse and Passover sufficient?
  4. Can Luke’s registration under Quirinius be placed securely within either proposed chronology?
  5. Does Luke’s “about thirty” meaningfully favor one birth range?
  6. Can the priestly division of Abijah be dated without assuming an unbroken schedule that the evidence does not preserve?
  7. Did the complete claimed astronomical configuration occur on September 11, 3 BC under documented settings?
  8. Was that configuration unique within the surrounding years, or did similar arrangements recur?
  9. Are astronomical alignments sufficient historical evidence, or do they only establish possibility?
  10. How much weight should Josephus carry where his regnal totals, narrative sequence, and later manuscript transmission must be reconciled?
  11. How should ancient Jewish celestial symbolism be established without importing modern constellation interpretations?
  12. Can multiple pieces of evidence coexist without all pointing to one exact date?
