What Should All Biblical Believers Be Called?

Scripture gives God’s people many covenant, communal, relational, and mission-shaped descriptions. This investigation asks whether one of them is commanded as the universal name.

Does Scripture prescribe one label for every faithful biblical believer, or does it use a family of names whose meaning depends on covenant, context, and calling?

Editorial note: Names can clarify belonging, but they can also smuggle later boundaries into the text. Hebrew and Greek terms are treated as evidence; modern denominational preferences are not allowed to decide the answer in advance.
Primary question

A name, a description, or a relationship?

The Bible does not use labels in only one way. Some identify a covenant people, some describe holiness or faith, some name a gathered assembly, some express family relationship, and some were applied by outsiders.

Method: Before asking which word “wins,” identify who uses it, whom it includes, whether it is commanded, and whether it functions as a proper name.
Textual catalog

Major biblical designations

Israel / children of Israel

Representative texts: Exodus 4:22; Deuteronomy 7:6

Function: Covenant people descended from and joined to Israel; context determines ethnic, national, remnant, or theological scope.

Evidence: Explicit

Assembly — qahal

Representative texts: Deuteronomy 31:30

Function: A gathered assembly; the Septuagint often renders the concept with ekklēsia.

Evidence: Explicit

Congregation — edah

Representative texts: Numbers 27:17

Function: The congregation/community of Israel.

Evidence: Explicit

People of YHWH / holy people

Representative texts: Deuteronomy 14:2

Function: Covenant identity grounded in God’s choosing and holiness.

Evidence: Explicit

Disciples

Representative texts: Acts 6:1–2

Function: The most frequent Acts label for followers learning and living under Yeshua.

Evidence: Explicit

Believers / faithful

Representative texts: Acts 5:14; Ephesians 1:1

Function: Describes trust, allegiance, or faithfulness rather than a formal denominational name.

Evidence: Explicit

Saints / holy ones

Representative texts: Romans 1:7; 1 Corinthians 1:2

Function: A communal holiness designation used broadly in the letters.

Evidence: Explicit

Brothers and sisters

Representative texts: Acts 15:23; James 2:1

Function: A relational family term; translations differ because Greek adelphoi can address a mixed group.

Evidence: Explicit

The Way

Representative texts: Acts 9:2; 19:9,23; 24:14,22

Function: An early movement designation in Acts.

Evidence: Explicit

Assembly / church — ekklēsia

Representative texts: Matthew 16:18; Acts 8:1

Function: The called or gathered assembly; “church” is an English rendering, not the original proper name.

Evidence: Explicit

Christians

Representative texts: Acts 11:26; 26:28; 1 Peter 4:16

Function: A rare label in the NT. Acts reports it; 1 Peter accepts suffering under it without commanding it as the sole name.

Evidence: Explicit, rare

Nazarenes

Representative texts: Acts 24:5

Function: An accusation by Tertullus against “the sect of the Nazarenes,” not a community naming command.

Evidence: External label

Three uses must not be confused

How labels function

Israel / people of God

Covenant identity

These terms locate people inside the biblical covenant story. Their scope and the inclusion of gentiles must be argued from context, not assumed from a modern label.

Brothers, sisters, children

Relational identity

These terms describe a family created by God and Messiah. They are frequent, but not normally formal movement names.

Disciples / believers / holy ones

Discipleship identity

These describe practice, allegiance, and calling. They can apply broadly without erasing Israel or local assemblies.

The Way / Christians / Nazarenes

Public movement labels

Acts records labels used in public settings. Reporting a name does not automatically establish a command that all later believers must use it.

The word Christian

What do the three occurrences prove?

Acts 11:26 says the disciples were first called Christians in Antioch. The passive wording does not identify who coined the label. Acts 26:28 places it on Agrippa’s lips. 1 Peter 4:16 tells a sufferer not to be ashamed if suffering “as a Christian.”

Supported: “Christian” is a legitimate NT-era designation. Not established: that it is the only permitted or divinely mandated universal name.
Israel and the nations

The hardest boundary question

Romans 9–11, Ephesians 2, Galatians 3, and 1 Peter 2 all describe the inclusion of people from the nations using Israel-shaped covenant language. They do not all answer the naming question with the same vocabulary.

Questions that must remain distinct
  • Who belongs to Messiah?
  • Who is Israel in a given passage?
  • How are gentiles joined to the covenant story?
  • What should a mixed local assembly call itself?
  • What label communicates faithfully in a modern setting?
Measured conclusion

What can be said without overclaiming?

Scripture repeatedly calls Messiah’s people disciples, believers, holy ones, brothers and sisters, assemblies, and the people of God. It also records “The Way” and “Christians.” No passage explicitly commands one English label as the exclusive name for every biblical believer.

A responsible community may choose a clear public label, but should not turn that practical choice into a command Scripture does not give.
