Who Was Paul—or Rabbi Shaul?

Paul is one of the most influential and most disputed figures in the New Testament. To understand his letters responsibly, we must ask who he was before deciding what we think he taught.

What can Scripture, Paul’s own letters, Acts, historical context, and later tradition responsibly tell us about Shaul of Tarsus—and how should that evidence shape the way we read him?

Editorial note: This investigation distinguishes Paul’s own testimony, the narrative of Acts, historical context, later tradition, and modern interpretation. The title uses “Rabbi Shaul” as a familiar Jewish-context description, but the New Testament does not explicitly assign Paul the formal title “rabbi.” Whether that title should be used historically is therefore an interpretive question, not an established fact.
Central question

Which Paul are we reading?

Paul is often presented through conclusions inherited from later theological debates: defender of Torah, opponent of Torah, founder of Christianity, apostle of grace, enemy of Judaism, missionary to the nations, or misunderstood Jewish teacher.

Some of these descriptions preserve important evidence. Others may flatten a complex first-century person into a slogan. Before deciding whether Paul opposed Moses, created a new religion, or remained faithfully inside Israel’s story, we need to examine the sources and identify where evidence ends and interpretation begins.

The investigation begins with a discipline: let Paul’s own letters speak first, let Acts provide an additional narrative witness, let historical context clarify the world around him, and let later tradition remain clearly labeled as later tradition.
Evidence map

1. What kinds of evidence do we have?

Paul is unusually well documented for an ancient religious figure, but the available sources do not all carry the same kind of weight.

Primary testimony

Paul’s letters

The letters attributed to Paul contain direct self-description, arguments, relationships, travel references, disputes, pastoral concerns, and interpretations of Scripture. Where authorship is widely disputed, that uncertainty should be stated rather than hidden.

Narrative witness

Acts of the Apostles

Acts narrates Paul’s calling, travels, trials, speeches, and relationships. It is a later narrative source within the New Testament and should be compared carefully with Paul’s own letters.

Historical context

Second Temple Judaism and Rome

Jewish interpretive traditions, synagogue life, Roman citizenship, urban networks, diaspora communities, and imperial law help explain the world in which Paul lived and wrote.

Later tradition

Martyrdom and memory

Early Christian tradition associates Paul’s death with Rome under Nero. The broad tradition is ancient, but many precise details cannot be independently verified.

Method: A statement from Paul about himself, a speech attributed to Paul in Acts, and a later tradition about Paul may all be useful—but they are not the same kind of evidence.
Historical identity

2. Shaul of Tarsus

The sources present Paul as a Jew from Tarsus in Cilicia who moved within both Jewish and Roman worlds.

Name

Acts uses both “Saul” and “Paul.” Shaul (שאול) is a Hebrew name; Paulos is Greek and Paulus is Latin. The text does not require a dramatic religious renaming. A person operating across Jewish and Roman settings could use more than one name.

Jewish identity

Paul identifies himself as an Israelite, from the tribe of Benjamin, a Hebrew of Hebrews, and a Pharisee (Romans 11:1; Philippians 3:5; Acts 23:6).

Roman status

Acts presents Paul as a Roman citizen and shows that citizenship affecting his treatment by officials, his legal protections, and his appeal to Caesar (Acts 16:37–39; 22:25–29; 25:10–12).

Place of origin

Acts identifies Tarsus in Cilicia as Paul’s city (Acts 21:39; 22:3). His life therefore belongs to the Jewish diaspora as well as to Judea and Jerusalem.

Should he be called “Rabbi Shaul”?

Paul was unquestionably a Jewish teacher and interpreter of Scripture. Acts also associates him with Pharisaic formation and with Gamaliel. However, the New Testament does not explicitly call him “Rabbi,” and later rabbinic titles and institutional forms should not be projected backward without care.

“Rav Shaul” may be useful as a modern reminder of Paul’s Jewish identity, but it should be presented as a descriptive choice rather than a formally documented ancient title.

Formation

3. A Pharisee formed inside Israel’s Scriptures

Paul was not a Gentile outsider who discovered the God of Israel late in life. His own testimony presents him as deeply formed by Jewish ancestry, zeal, Torah, and Pharisaic interpretation.

Paul describes himself as “circumcised the eighth day, of the people of Israel, of the tribe of Benjamin, a Hebrew of Hebrews; concerning the law, a Pharisee” (Philippians 3:5).

Acts records Paul saying that he was educated “at the feet of Gamaliel” and instructed according to the ancestral law (Acts 22:3). Acts 23:6 further presents him identifying himself as “a Pharisee, a son of Pharisees.” These passages do not answer every question about the precise form of his education, but they strongly place him within Jewish interpretive and covenantal debates.

What does “blameless concerning the law” mean?

In Philippians 3:6, Paul describes himself as “blameless” concerning righteousness in the law. This should not automatically be read as a claim of sinless perfection. It may describe recognized faithfulness within his inherited interpretive and communal framework.

Interpretation remains necessary. The statement tells us that Paul did not remember his pre-Messiah life as casual, ignorant, or indifferent toward Torah.

Strongly supported: Paul’s arguments emerged from inside Jewish Scripture and Jewish controversy. Whatever changed after his encounter with Yeshua, he did not begin as a detached critic of Israel’s God or Israel’s story.
Turning point

4. The Damascus encounter

Paul’s life changed because he became convinced that the crucified Yeshua had been raised and revealed to him.

Acts narrates the encounter three times, with differences in emphasis for different settings (Acts 9, 22, and 26). Paul’s letters do not repeat the full road narrative, but they do affirm that he had seen the risen Messiah, received a calling, and moved from persecuting the assembly to proclaiming the faith he once tried to destroy (1 Corinthians 9:1; 15:8–10; Galatians 1:13–24).

Zeal against the movement

Paul recalls persecuting the assembly of God and trying to destroy it.

Revelation of Yeshua

Paul becomes convinced that the risen Yeshua is Israel’s Messiah and his own Lord.

Calling among the nations

Paul understands his mission as carrying the good news among Gentiles while remaining bound to Israel’s promises.

Conflict and reinterpretation

His former convictions are not simply discarded; they are reorganized around the Messiah, resurrection, and the inclusion of the nations.

Conversion, calling, or both?

Many Christian traditions describe the Damascus experience as Paul’s conversion from Judaism to Christianity. Other scholars argue that “calling” is more historically precise because Paul continued to identify as an Israelite and understood his mission through Israel’s Scriptures and prophetic vocation.

The evidence clearly supports a radical transformation. Whether “conversion” or “calling” best describes that transformation depends partly on how one defines Judaism, Christianity, and religious identity in the first century.

Mission and communities

5. What did Paul actually do?

Paul became a traveling apostle, teacher, community founder, letter writer, and witness to Yeshua among the nations.

  • He proclaimed Yeshua as Messiah and Lord. His message centered on the death, resurrection, reign, and coming of the Messiah.
  • He focused heavily on Gentile inclusion. Paul understood himself as specially commissioned to the nations, while continuing to address Jews and Israel’s promises.
  • He formed assemblies. These were communities meeting in homes and other available spaces—not later institutional church buildings.
  • He trained leaders and maintained relationships. His letters reveal conflict, affection, correction, fundraising, suffering, travel plans, and pastoral responsibility.
  • He interpreted Israel’s Scriptures. Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings form the scriptural world of his arguments.

Several letters are broadly recognized as early witnesses to Paul’s own voice: Romans, 1 and 2 Corinthians, Galatians, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, and Philemon. Other letters attributed to Paul remain debated in modern scholarship. Faith communities may receive the full traditional Pauline collection while still acknowledging the historical authorship discussion.

Historical significance: Paul’s letters are among the earliest surviving writings of the Yeshua movement. They allow readers to encounter disputes and community problems while the movement was still developing inside the first-century Jewish and Roman world.
The central controversy

6. Did Paul leave Judaism or begin a new religion?

Paul never writes, “I left Judaism and founded Christianity.” That does not settle every historical question, but it should caution us against reading later religious boundaries directly into his self-understanding.

Paul continues to call himself an Israelite (Romans 11:1), speaks of Israel’s covenants and promises (Romans 9:4–5), reasons from Abraham, Moses, the Prophets, and the Psalms, and expects God’s faithfulness to Israel to remain central to the story. At the same time, his mission creates communities in which uncircumcised Gentiles are welcomed as full participants through the Messiah.

Traditional Christian reading

A common traditional reading understands Paul as teaching a decisive transition from the Mosaic covenant to a new covenant community defined by faith in Messiah rather than Torah observance. This view often distinguishes moral instruction from covenantal or ceremonial obligations.

New Perspective readings

New Perspective approaches generally challenge the idea that first-century Judaism was simply a religion of works-righteousness. They often read Paul’s disputes about “works of the law” in relation to covenant identity, boundary markers, and the inclusion of Gentiles rather than as a rejection of Jewish obedience itself.

Paul within Judaism readings

Paul within Judaism approaches argue that Paul remained a Jewish apostle whose instructions to Gentiles should not automatically be treated as instructions for Jews to abandon Torah. These readings emphasize differentiated callings within one eschatological people of God.

Messianic and Torah-positive readings

Messianic Jewish and Torah-positive interpreters often emphasize continuity between Paul, Yeshua, Torah, and the prophetic hope of Israel. Within these communities, substantial disagreements remain about covenant obligation, Gentile practice, Jewish identity, and how Paul’s warnings should be applied.

Current assessment: The evidence strongly supports reading Paul as a Jewish follower of Yeshua arguing about the nations, covenant membership, faithfulness, and the age of Messiah. Whether his teaching amounts to a new religion depends partly on later historical developments and on the definition of “religion.”
Key vocabulary

7. Why Paul is so easy to misunderstand

Paul’s letters are difficult partly because a single translated word may carry several possible meanings, and because modern readers often inherit definitions from later debates.

Faith · πίστις · אֱמוּנָה

Depending on context, “faith” may involve belief, trust, loyalty, faithfulness, or allegiance. Reducing it to mental agreement can make Paul’s ethical expectations appear disconnected from his gospel.

Law · νόμος · Torah

“Law” may refer to Torah, a particular command, Scripture more broadly, a legal principle, the power of sin and death, or a disputed use of Torah. Every occurrence must be read in context.

Works of the law

Interpreters disagree whether the phrase primarily concerns human merit, Jewish identity practices, sectarian interpretations, covenant membership, or some combination.

Flesh and Spirit

Paul’s contrast does not simply mean body versus soul. It often concerns rival modes of life, allegiance, desire, mortality, and the transforming work of God’s Spirit.

Reading discipline: Before declaring that Paul praised or abolished “the law,” ask what law, whose use of it, in which argument, for which audience, and toward what purpose.
Torah and Messiah

8. Was Paul anti-Torah?

Paul can sound deeply positive about Torah in one passage and fiercely critical in another. Any responsible conclusion must account for both.

Positive witness

Torah as holy and good

Paul calls the law holy and the commandment holy, righteous, and good (Romans 7:12). He appeals to Torah repeatedly for ethics, theology, worship, community order, and the identity of Messiah.

Critical witness

Torah in relation to sin and condemnation

Paul argues that law can expose transgression, intensify accountability, and become entangled with sin and death when human beings remain enslaved to sin.

Gentile question

Must Gentiles become Jews?

Galatians and Romans address whether Gentiles must receive circumcision and take on Jewish identity as the basis of covenant membership. Paul’s answer places belonging in Messiah rather than ethnic conversion.

Interpretive question

What remains disputed?

Readers disagree about whether Paul changes Torah’s authority, changes its covenantal function, applies it differently to Jews and Gentiles, or interprets its goal through Messiah.

The simple statement “Paul was anti-Torah” cannot carry the full evidence. Neither can the statement “Paul changed nothing.” His letters present a Messiah-centered rereading of Israel’s Scriptures in response to resurrection, the Spirit, and the entrance of Gentiles into the people of Israel’s God.

Identity tension

9. The tensions Paul holds together

Paul is difficult to read because his identity and mission hold several worlds together at once.

  • Jewish Pharisee: formed by ancestral traditions and Israel’s Scriptures.
  • Follower of Yeshua: convinced that the crucified Messiah had been raised and enthroned.
  • Apostle to the nations: called to announce Israel’s Messiah among Gentiles.
  • Interpreter of Torah: arguing from Scripture rather than discarding it.
  • Apocalyptic thinker: expecting the present age to be transformed by God’s approaching reign.
  • Roman citizen: moving through imperial roads, cities, courts, and legal structures.
  • Community shepherd: addressing money, sex, conflict, food, worship, suffering, leadership, and reconciliation.
This tension is not a flaw in the evidence. It may be the central reason Paul cannot be reduced to either “Jewish teacher” or “Christian theologian” without qualification.
Death and later tradition

10. What do we know about Paul’s death?

The New Testament does not narrate Paul’s death. Later Christian tradition places his martyrdom in Rome, commonly during Nero’s reign in the 60s CE.

The broad martyrdom tradition is early and widely received. The precise date, legal proceedings, method, and final sequence of Paul’s travels are less certain. These details should therefore be described as tradition or reconstruction rather than explicit Scripture.

Confidence level: Paul’s suffering, imprisonment, and expectation of possible death are explicit in the letters and Acts. His execution in Rome is strongly traditional; its exact details remain only partially verifiable.
Current assessment

Who was Paul—or Rabbi Shaul?

Paul was a Jew from Tarsus, an Israelite of Benjamin, a Pharisee, a Roman citizen according to Acts, a persecutor of the early Yeshua movement, and then an apostle who became convinced that Yeshua had been raised as Messiah.

He did not understand himself as abandoning Israel’s God or Israel’s Scriptures. His mission was to announce that Israel’s Messiah had acted for Israel and the nations, and to form communities in which Gentiles could belong without becoming ethnically Jewish.

His writings should therefore be read neither as detached Greek philosophy nor as a simple rejection of Moses. They are Jewish, scriptural, messianic, pastoral, apocalyptic, and shaped by the urgent problem of how Jews and Gentiles could live faithfully together in light of the Messiah.

Version 1 assessment: Paul is best approached as a Jewish apostle of Yeshua whose letters represent an intense internal argument about Torah, Messiah, covenant, resurrection, Israel, and the nations. The title “Rabbi Shaul” may remind modern readers of that Jewish setting, but it should not be used to erase uncertainty or to impose a later formal title upon him.
Questions Still Open

Questions Still Open

  1. How should Paul’s own letters and the narrative of Acts be weighed when details appear difficult to harmonize?
  2. Which letters preserve Paul’s direct voice, and how should disputed authorship affect interpretation?
  3. Did Paul expect Jewish followers of Yeshua to continue Torah observance differently from Gentile followers?
  4. What exactly did Paul mean by “works of the law” in each argument?
  5. How should Romans 7, Galatians 3, Ephesians 2, and Colossians 2 be read alongside Paul’s positive statements about Torah?
  6. Does “faith in Messiah” or “the faithfulness of Messiah” best represent key Pauline phrases—or can both ideas be present?
  7. How much authority should later Christian interpretations carry when reconstructing Paul’s first-century intent?
  8. Is “Rabbi Shaul” a helpful corrective to anti-Jewish readings, or does it risk claiming more historical precision than the evidence allows?
  9. How did Paul understand the relationship among the house of Israel, the house of Judah, the Jewish people, and believing Gentiles?
  10. Where has Christian history interpreted Paul in ways that Paul himself might not recognize?
The HomeGroups invitation

Read Paul with Paul—not merely through inherited conclusions

Paul does not need to be rescued by ignoring his difficult passages, and he should not be condemned through slogans that bypass his Jewish context.

Read his letters in full. Compare them with Acts. Trace his quotations and echoes back into Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings. Define his vocabulary carefully. Distinguish what Paul explicitly says from what later readers conclude. Let disagreement remain visible where the evidence remains disputed.
