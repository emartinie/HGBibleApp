Non-Canonical Sources Quoted or Echoed in the New Testament

The New Testament’s authors did not write in a literary vacuum. They knew Israel’s Scriptures, Jewish interpretive traditions, Second Temple works, Greek poetry, and stories circulating beyond surviving books.

What exactly did the New Testament authors know and use—and how can direct quotation, literary dependence, shared tradition, and coincidence be kept distinct?

Editorial note: This deep dive is extracted from INV-016. It is intentionally dataset-shaped, but remains an Investigation until Greek-level comparison and manuscript metadata are completed.
Answer in brief

What is certain?

At minimum, the New Testament directly quotes 1 Enoch and Greek poetry, names Jewish figures absent from the Exodus account, and reflects traditions also preserved in Second Temple literature. The difficult question is not whether external material appears, but which literary relationship best explains each case.

Direct quotation

The strongest cases

NTX-001

Jude 14–15 / 1 Enoch 1:9

Jude names Enoch and reproduces distinctive judgment language. This is the clearest Jewish non-canonical quotation in the NT.

NTX-007

Acts 17:28 / Aratus

Paul’s “we too are his offspring” corresponds to Aratus’ Phaenomena. The quotation is redirected toward the Creator rather than used to endorse the poem’s whole theology.

NTX-009

1 Corinthians 15:33 / Menander

A Greek comic maxim is used inside Paul’s resurrection argument. Quotation does not equal canonization.

NTX-010

Titus 1:12 / Cretan poet

The attribution to “one of their own prophets” is explicit; identifying the exact lost poem is less certain.

Jude as a test case

One letter, several kinds of source use

Jude contains a direct Enoch quotation (vv. 14–15), an angelic dispute known from a tradition associated by Origen with the Assumption of Moses (v. 9), Watchers material (v. 6), and imagery resembling Enochic wandering stars (v. 13).

Method lesson: the presence of one certain quotation does not automatically make every parallel in the same letter a quotation—or canonize the quoted work as a whole.

Primary comparison: Jude; 1 Enoch overview and text; Origen, De Principiis 3.2.1.

Literary dependence test

Before calling something an echo

  1. Verbal agreement: Are rare words or an ordered phrase shared in the relevant language?
  2. Distinctive concept: Is the idea unusual enough to identify a source?
  3. Sequence: Do several images occur in the same order?
  4. Availability: Could the candidate source plausibly predate and reach the NT author?
  5. Direction: Is the candidate source earlier, or could it depend on the NT?
  6. Common tradition: Could both texts draw from a third oral or written tradition?
  7. Authorial signal: Does the writer name a poet, prophet, scripture, or saying?
Canon question

What quotation does and does not mean

It can mean

  • The author expected the audience to recognize the material.
  • The cited line was useful or judged true in context.
  • The author participated in a wider Jewish or Greco-Roman discourse.

It does not automatically mean

  • The entire source is inspired.
  • The source belongs in every biblical canon.
  • Every narrative detail in the source is historically affirmed.
  • The author copied rather than shared a tradition.
Intertext handoff

What the future engine will need

Each NTX record is designed to become a machine-readable edge:

  • from_passage and to_source
  • relation_type: quotation, allusion, echo, tradition, attribution, disputed
  • source_language and verbal_overlap
  • confidence plus a written rationale
  • alternatives and direction_of_dependence
  • earliest_witness and manuscript date

No record should enter the Intertext engine as a fact without retaining its uncertainty.
